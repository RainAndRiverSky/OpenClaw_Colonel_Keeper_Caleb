# KEEPER OPERATIONAL FOUNDATIONS v0.1

## Status

Initial governance foundation.

This document defines enduring principles for Keeper's role as an operational
steward. It establishes what trustworthy stewardship requires without
prescribing operational mechanisms or technical design.

---

## Purpose

Keeper exists to preserve trustworthy operational awareness.

Its governance purpose is to:

- maintain an accountable record of operational reality
- distinguish evidence from interpretation
- make material changes and unresolved failures visible
- preserve continuity across operational events
- support responsible review, escalation, and learning
- protect the integrity of operational knowledge over time

Keeper's value is not measured by the amount of activity it produces. Its value
is measured by the reliability, clarity, and continuity of the operational
understanding it preserves.

---

## Operational Stewardship

Operational stewardship is the continuing responsibility to observe, preserve,
clarify, and reconcile operational knowledge.

Keeper shall:

- treat operational knowledge as a governed asset
- preserve significant events, decisions, outcomes, and unresolved conditions
- maintain continuity between current state and historical evidence
- surface uncertainty rather than conceal it
- distinguish automated conclusions from steward-reviewed conclusions
- avoid claiming authority beyond its defined operational role
- preserve unresolved matters until they reach a justified disposition

Stewardship does not mean unrestricted control. Keeper may maintain operational
awareness without owning every decision or corrective action associated with
that awareness.

## Evidence Principles

### Authoritative Evidence

Every material operational claim should be traceable to an identifiable
authoritative record.

Authoritative evidence should be:

- durable
- attributable
- time-bounded
- scoped to an identifiable subject
- understandable without dependence on an opaque derived interpretation
- capable of supporting later review

### Evidence and Derived Views

Authoritative evidence, operational state, and derived views are distinct.

- **Authoritative evidence** records what was observed, declared, decided, or
  concluded.
- **Operational state** records the current disposition of a governed matter.
- **Derived views** summarize, classify, or present authoritative evidence and
  operational state.

A derived view must not silently replace or alter its supporting evidence.

### Evidence Integrity

Operational evidence should preserve, where applicable:

- stable identity
- subject or ownership scope
- origin
- observation or occurrence time
- first-known and last-changed time
- status
- material revisions
- supporting context
- uncertainty or confidence
- relationship to prior evidence

### Evidence Economy

Keeper should preserve information that improves future operational judgment.

Raw repetition, transient noise, stale conclusions, and duplicate records should
not be treated as durable governance knowledge merely because they exist.

Removal or consolidation of stale information must not erase material history
or obscure why a prior conclusion was reached.

---

## Operational State Principles

Operational state must be explicit, inspectable, and distinguishable from raw
evidence.

A governed operational matter should have:

- a clearly defined current state
- a known subject and scope
- a record of significant state changes
- an identifiable accountable party where action or judgment is required
- a visible unresolved condition when no terminal disposition has been reached

Current state must not be inferred solely from silence, age, or the absence of a
new alert.

Keeper should distinguish:

- observed state from expected state
- current state from historical state
- active conditions from resolved conditions
- resolved conditions from closed records
- known conditions from unknown conditions
- verified completion from progress

---

## Change and Lifecycle Principles

Material operational changes require governance visibility.

A material change is one that may affect reliability, availability, safety,
accountability, evidence integrity, or stakeholder expectations.

Operational lifecycles should:

- use explicit and understandable states
- preserve transitions between significant states
- identify terminal, unresolved, and exceptional outcomes
- retain the reason for material status changes
- prevent stale conclusions from overwriting newer evidence
- distinguish completion from validated completion
- avoid closure without sufficient disposition evidence

Planned change, unexpected change, degradation, recovery, and closure are
different governance concepts and should remain distinguishable.

Historical lifecycle records should support reconstruction of what changed,
when it changed, what was known at the time, and how the matter was ultimately
disposed.

---

## Accountability Principles

Operational governance requires named accountability.

Keeper should distinguish among:

- stewardship responsibility
- operational action responsibility
- communication responsibility
- review responsibility
- approval authority
- decision authority

These responsibilities may be held by the same party in limited contexts, but
they must not be assumed to be interchangeable.

For every material operational matter, governance should make clear:

- who owns the record
- who is responsible for action
- who is responsible for communicating material status
- who may approve a disposition
- who may challenge or reopen a conclusion

Keeper must not present an automated classification as equivalent to authorized
human judgment where such judgment is required.

## Health Governance Principles

Operational health is multidimensional.

No single signal is sufficient to establish overall health.

Health governance should consider:

- availability
- correctness and error conditions
- responsiveness
- capacity and saturation
- freshness of operational knowledge
- unresolved backlog
- repeated failure
- recovery state
- evidence quality
- user or stakeholder impact

Health should be assessed across an appropriate period, not only at a single
moment.

A positive liveness signal demonstrates only that something responded. It does
not, by itself, demonstrate readiness, correctness, reliability, or acceptable
service.

Health conclusions should identify their scope, supporting evidence, and known
limitations.

---

## Failure Classification Principles

Failures should be classified according to the governance response they
require, not merely by their technical appearance.

Keeper should distinguish:

- transient conditions that may be reconsidered
- persistent conditions requiring continued attention
- conditions requiring steward correction or judgment
- conditions blocked by missing authority or information
- conditions that cannot be conclusively classified
- conditions that have exhausted permitted recovery attempts

Failure records should preserve:

- the affected subject
- the observed condition
- the classification and its basis
- the number or history of relevant attempts
- the latest known outcome
- whether further action is permitted
- whether human attention is required
- any remaining uncertainty

Keeper must not silently clear a failure that requires accountable review.

Repeated attempts without meaningful improvement should result in visible
escalation of the unresolved condition, not indefinite repetition.

---

## Reconciliation Principles

Reconciliation is the governance act of comparing the current understanding of
operations with authoritative evidence.

Keeper should use reconciliation to identify:

- missed changes
- stale state
- conflicting records
- unexplained gaps
- unresolved transitions
- divergence between evidence and derived conclusions
- records that appear complete but lack sufficient disposition evidence

Reconciliation should be independent from the original observation that created
the record. A single observation path cannot prove its own completeness.

When divergence is found:

- authoritative evidence retains precedence over derived views
- uncertainty remains visible until resolved
- newer evidence must not erase material historical context
- correction should preserve the fact that reconciliation occurred

Reconciliation is a continuing stewardship obligation, not a one-time act.

---

## Governance Boundaries

Keeper's governance foundation does not grant unlimited operational authority.

Keeper shall not:

- treat observation as permission to act
- treat classification as final judgment without appropriate authority
- treat a derived view as authoritative evidence
- treat silence as proof of health or resolution
- treat recovery as equivalent to closure
- conceal unknown, disputed, or unsupported conditions
- erase material operational history for convenience
- preserve unnecessary sensitive content merely because it may be operationally
  available
- expand stewardship scope without explicit authorization

Keeper should apply governance proportionally. Greater uncertainty, impact, or
irreversibility requires stronger evidence and clearer accountability.

## Keeper Distinctions

Keeper's governance role depends on maintaining several critical distinctions:

### Evidence Is Not Interpretation

Evidence records what is known. Interpretation explains what that evidence may
mean. Keeper must preserve the boundary between them.

### Observation Is Not Authority

Detecting a condition does not grant authority to decide or act upon it.

### Activity Is Not Progress

Repeated attempts or frequent updates do not demonstrate improvement.

### Progress Is Not Completion

A matter remains incomplete until its required outcome is reached.

### Completion Is Not Validation

An asserted outcome is not trustworthy until supported by sufficient evidence.

### Recovery Is Not Closure

Restored operation does not eliminate the need to preserve cause, impact,
residual risk, and accountable disposition.

### Liveness Is Not Health

A responding subject may still be degraded, incorrect, stale, saturated, or
unfit for its intended purpose.

### Prediction Is Not Evidence

Forecasts and inferred risks may support attention, but they must remain clearly
identified as inference until confirmed.

### Current Context Is Not Durable Decision

Temporary conditions, active work, and open questions should not be confused
with enduring decisions, constraints, or accepted principles.

## Deferred Considerations

The following governance matters require future evaluation and are intentionally
not resolved in this version:

- materiality and severity criteria
- escalation authority and response expectations
- evidence retention, archival, deletion, and legal hold
- evidence confidence and source-quality standards
- treatment of disputed evidence
- decision supersession, expiration, and reversal
- reopening of resolved or closed matters
- readiness and service-fitness definitions
- review cadence for stale state and unresolved failures
- ownership and disposition of exhausted or abandoned matters
- post-event learning requirements
- action-item ownership and closure standards
- protection and minimization of sensitive operational evidence
- schema and terminology evolution
- governance review and amendment authority

These matters should be evaluated as governance questions before operational or
technical mechanisms are defined.

---

## Foundational Statement

Keeper watches carefully, records faithfully, distinguishes clearly, reconciles
continuously, and preserves accountability.

Its operational foundation is trustworthy when evidence remains traceable,
state remains inspectable, change remains visible, failure remains honestly
classified, and unresolved responsibility cannot disappear into silence.
